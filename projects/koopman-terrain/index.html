Modelling Quadruped Leg Dynamics on Deformable Terrains Using Data-Driven Koopman Operators

Presented Krolicki et al.'s switched-Koopman approach to terrain-aware quadruped leg modelling, reproduced its core findings through an independent simulation across three deformable terrains and three Koopman dictionaries, and critically evaluated the paper's methodology — identifying concrete gaps in its reproducibility, assumptions, and robustness.

Problem Context

Quadruped robots are used in search and rescue, agriculture, inspection, and exploration — settings where the terrain underfoot is rarely known in advance. Reliable legged locomotion depends on accurately predicting how the ground will react to contact, but this is fundamentally terrain-dependent, and existing approaches each trade off accuracy for cost or generality:

  • Sensor-based terrain sensing (Cong et al., Hutter et al.) — direct contact force computation, but vulnerable to sensor degradation.
  • State estimation methods (Sebastian et al., Fink et al.) — model-based observer estimation needing no extra sensors, but degrades on soft outdoor terrain.
  • Spring-damper models (Zhang et al., Bledt et al.) — simple and intuitive, but can't capture the full nonlinearity of robot-terrain interaction.
  • Neural network-based methods (Cong et al., Hu & Xiong) — capture nonlinear interactions, but require extensive training data and lack interpretability.

The paper at the center of this presentation — Krolicki et al. (2022) — proposes a fifth approach: modelling the leg as a switched dynamical system and using data-driven Koopman operators to approximate its dynamics directly from joint states and torques, without dedicated terrain sensors.

Core Method

A quadruped leg doesn't behave as a single consistent dynamical system — its dynamics change depending on whether the leg is in flight (no ground contact) or stance (ground reaction forces acting), which means it can't be accurately approximated by a single Koopman operator. The paper instead models the leg as a switched dynamical system, with the model switching between flight and stance dynamics based on leg height.

For each mode, the Koopman operator is approximated from data using EDMDc (Extended Dynamic Mode Decomposition with control): snapshot data is generated from the system, a dictionary of observable functions is chosen, and the Koopman matrices are constructed from that snapshot data. Once approximated, eigen analysis of the resulting Koopman generator reveals terrain-dependent structure in its spectrum — in principle allowing terrain type to be inferred from the eigenvalues alone, with no additional sensors beyond joint encoders and torque estimates.

Simulation & Methodology

To evaluate the approach, the full pipeline was implemented and tested in simulation, following the modelling → data collection → switched system → Koopman operator → eigen analysis sequence:

  • Dynamic & kinematic modelling: trajectory generation feeds an inverse kinematics block producing target joint angles, tracked by a PD controller; forward dynamics and the leg Jacobian relate desired and actual torque to ground reaction forces, with reaction forces estimated from motor current.
  • Data collection: separate datasets were collected for stance (ground reaction present, across terrains — sand, mulch, soil, pebble sand) and flight (no ground reaction, across multiple gaits).
  • Terrain simulation: three deformable terrain behaviours were modelled — mud-like (strong nonlinear resistance that increases with depth and force-saturates), sand-like (granular media exhibiting quadratic and linear stiffness), and a third mud-variant that compresses easily and plateaus due to limited load-bearing capacity — with random Gaussian noise added to simulate slip and depth-measurement uncertainty.
  • Koopman dictionary comparison: three dictionary choices were tested for the EDMDc approximation — linear terms only, up to quadratic terms, and a sinusoidal (sine/cosine) dictionary — to compare reconstruction accuracy.

Conclusions (from the paper)

  • The switched Koopman model accurately predicts leg dynamics across deformable terrains.
  • Terrain properties appear clearly in the generator spectrum and eigenfunctions.
  • Soft terrains shift eigenvalues toward the real axis, indicating increased damping; stiff terrains retain oscillatory modes.
  • These spectral signatures enable terrain classification without dedicated terrain sensors.
  • The approach requires only joint states and torques — lightweight, data-driven, and scalable.

Critical Analysis

Beyond presenting and reproducing the method in simulation, a key part of this project was critically evaluating the original paper's claims and identifying gaps that limit its practical reproducibility and robustness:

  • Reproducibility: the dictionary functions used in the original paper aren't reported, making the results difficult to reproduce exactly.
  • Chicken-and-egg problem: terrain identification depends on controller stability, but controller stability itself may depend on already knowing the terrain — a circular dependency the paper doesn't address.
  • Data preprocessing: left unspecified; poor filtering is known to produce unstable Koopman spectra, which directly undermines the eigenvalue-based terrain classification claim.
  • Data sufficiency: no guidance is given on how much training data is required, or whether a single training session generalises.
  • Robustness: no tests against impulsive or highly irregular terrain (e.g. pebbles), which are a realistic and more challenging case than the smooth deformable terrains studied.
  • Dictionary sensitivity: no sensitivity analysis with respect to dictionary size or composition.
  • Real-time feasibility: no discussion of the computational constraints involved in running this terrain identification approach in real time on hardware.

Connection to Ongoing Work

This project's switched-Koopman terrain modelling approach is the conceptual basis for the sensor-less terrain perception now being integrated into my M.Tech thesis quadruped leg platform — extending the method from simulation-only study and critique toward validation on physical hardware.
